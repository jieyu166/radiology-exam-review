(A) Currently, SBRT is the standard of care for early-stage node-negative NSCLC (T1N0M0 and T2N0M0) in patients who are not surgical candidates and in those who decline surgery.
(B) Late radiation fibrosis can demonstrate a well-defined mass-like consolidation, which can be larger than the original tumor and lacks features like air bronchograms and straight margins.
(C) The CT features of late radiation fibrosis after SBRT often continue to evolve for more than 4 years after lung SBRT.
(D) The presence of interstitial lung disease on the pretreatment images, especially honeycombing, has been associated with an increased risk of radiation pneumonitis.
??
**Ans: C**
RadioGraphics 2018; 38:1312–1336

- can continue to evolve > 2 years after lung SBRT, although evolution after 4 years has rarely been described.
- modified conventional pattern can further evolve into a scarlike or masslike fibrosis at follow-up, mimicks a recurrence.
